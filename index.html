Background

About

I am a Postdoctoral Research Fellow and Michigan Pioneer Fellow in the Department of Psychiatry at the University of Michigan (Dr. Soo-Eun Chang's Speech Neurophysiology Lab), where I investigate the neural bases of stuttering using structural and functional MRI, diffusion-weighted imaging, EEG, and noninvasive neuromodulation (tACS, tDCS, LIFU).

I completed my Ph.D. and M.Sc. in Biomedical Engineering at Boston University (Dr. Cara Stepp's Lab), where my dissertation focused on sensorimotor characterisation and neurocomputational modelling of speech motor control in Parkinson's disease and hyperfunctional voice disorders.

My broader goal is to develop neuromodulatory therapeutics that enhance quality of life for clinical populations with voice and speech disorders, bridging basic neuroscience with translational rehabilitation engineering.

Current Work

Projects

🧠

Neural Bases of Stuttering

Investigating structural and functional connectivity differences in adults and children who stutter using multimodal MRI (fMRI, DTI, fixel-based analysis) and connectivity modelling in Dr. Soo-Eun Chang's lab at UMich.

⚡

Noninvasive Neuromodulation

Developing and applying tACS, tDCS, and low-intensity focused ultrasound stimulation (LIFU) protocols to modulate neural oscillatory synchrony and speech motor circuits, with translational applications for stuttering and motor speech disorders.

📐

Neurocomputational Modelling

Developing biologically-grounded models of speech motor control, including LaDIVA — a neurocomputational model extending the DIVA framework to include laryngeal motor control for speech acquisition and production.

🔬

Sensorimotor Control in Parkinson's Disease

Characterising auditory and somatosensory feedback mechanisms of laryngeal and articulatory motor control in individuals with Parkinson's disease, including effects of dopaminergic medication on voice and speech control.

Teaching & Supervision

Mentoring

Mentoring is a core part of my academic identity. I believe that good mentorship means meeting students where they are — whether that is helping them run their first experiment, interpret a complex dataset, or navigate the path to graduate school and beyond.

My approach is guided by three principles: curiosity — encouraging students to ask their own questions; rigour — building strong technical and analytical foundations; and care — supporting the whole person, not just the project.

I have mentored students across a range of contexts, from hands-on research assistantships embedded in my PhD projects, to year-long structured programmes supporting undergraduates as they explore research, higher studies, and career paths in science.
